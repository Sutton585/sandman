## Context

In a fast-paced startup environment, I led UX and product design for a healthcare staffing platform built to simplify job scheduling and order fulfillment. The original MVP—built by an external team—was expensive, poorly designed, and functionally inadequate. I was brought in to rebuild it from the ground up.

**The Goal**

Address post-COVID staffing instability and coordination inefficiencies with an app that streamlines agency workflows and connects healthcare employers with qualified professionals.

**The Challenge**

- Launch two brands (Medical Staffing + General Staffing) and an MVP in under 6 months.
- No design systems, roadmaps, stakeholder alignment, workflows, or delivery process.
- Engineering was scaling rapidly with no product leadership or strategic vision.
- Departmental collaboration was siloed and informal.

**My Role & Approach**

As the first UX/Product hire, I set out to:

- Build trust across teams and stakeholders.
- Establish collaborative systems, processes, and documentation.
- Anchor product strategy around user needs.
- Model the kind of communication-driven, transparent workflows we needed org-wide.

---

## Strategic Process & Systems Design

### Tools & Systems

- Built a centralized **design system in Figma**, organized by atomic principles.
- Created a live **documentation hub** linking assets, requirements, usage notes, Jira tickets, and test results.
- Embedded usability testing into sprint planning and product roadmapping.
- Cut our design-to-dev cycle in half through rapid iteration from low-fi to high-fi.

### Framework & Methodology

Implemented **dual-track agile** to run continuous discovery and delivery in parallel.

- **Discovery Track**: Workshops, interviews, rapid prototyping, competitive analysis.
- **Delivery Track**: Sprint-based dev with constant design validation.

**Impact**

- Improved transparency across teams.
- Eliminated bottlenecks.
- Established a shared source of truth across disciplines.

---

## Stakeholder Collaboration & Engagement

### Community & Client Involvement

Real clients were involved early in prototyping and feedback cycles for critical flows.

This strengthened relationships, provided real-world validation, and supported sales strategy.

**Approach**

- Conducted workshops, interviews, and competitive analysis to uncover pain points.
- Integrated clients into the product development process to build loyalty and excitement.

**Insights**

**Pain Points**

- Redundant data entry and high error rates.
- Manual, email-based workflows delayed staffing.
- Poor UX from competitors made onboarding slow and painful.

**Response**

- Designed workflows to mirror users' existing behavior.
- Prioritized automation and flexibility.
- Created user flows that minimized training time and friction.

---

## Feature Spotlight: Large Order Scheduling

### The Challenge

As shift patterns became more complex, it was nearly impossible to schedule large job orders efficiently using legacy tools.

### The Solution

- **Spreadsheet-style interface** mimicked familiar workflows.
- **Auto-fill patterns** streamlined repetitive scheduling.
- **Customizable templates** allowed quick adaptation to varied shift needs.

### Impact

- Became the platform’s most praised and most used feature.
- Cut time spent scheduling by over 50%.
- Surfaced new opportunities to improve cross-department processes.

## Reflection & Results

This project demonstrates my approach to **user-centered, civic-minded design**:

- Scaling systems in resource-constrained environments.
- Building transparent, collaborative, and participatory processes.
- Centering users and community voices in all decision-making.

**Results**

- Average user satisfaction increased from **52% to 96%**.
- Key task completion times were cut by **50%**.
- The platform is still in use today, powered by the systems I implemented.
- Described by users as a “**game changer**” in their daily operations.

This is the kind of mission-driven work I aim to keep doing—equity-driven, community-centered, and built to scale.
